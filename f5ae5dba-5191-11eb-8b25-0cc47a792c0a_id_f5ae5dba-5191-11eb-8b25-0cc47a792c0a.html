Seoul Hackathon
Before I started coding, I vaguely assumed that it was too hard to learn and that it was merely boring.
Today, I would say that this is definitely not true. Out of the various activities I do, coding is one of my
all time favorite passion. For those who may not know, according to Wikipedia, coding is “the process of
designing and building an executable computer program to accomplish a specific computing result or to
perform a specific task.”
Recently, I participated in the Seoul Hackathon where students from around the world compete against
each other to create a winning application. This year, the judge was Jintae Kim, an associate professor of
electronic engineering at Konkuk University. Seoul Hackathon is an annual coding competition in which
graphic designers and programmers collaborate to create an application of their choice based on a given
prompt. The theme of the competition changes every year: this year’s prompt was to create an application
that addresses a COVID-19 related issue.
To get started, I formed a team with friends from my school who are experienced coders. We
brainstormed the best route to take in order to tackle the prompt the right (and best) way. After multiple
discussions, one of my teammates suggested that we create an application that teaches senior citizens how
to use video-calling applications, such as Google Meet, Skype, and Zoom, so that they can talk to their
children and grandchildren. This idea mainly stemmed from the fact that many seniors have trouble
navigating such applications, which are vital for them to connect with their relatives and receive
emergency help if necessary.
I was assigned the role of the back-end developer. Essentially, my role was to type out the entire code and
make sure that the application is user-friendly. I utilized Javascript, the coding language that I am the
most familiar with. On the other hand, my teammates served as front-end developers. Their job was to
create a suitable design for the application.
[Image of My Application’s home screen, Help Grandpa. Photo courtesy of Sean Kang]
The main screen of our app, “Help Grandpa,” the user is required to enter his/her name. This is followed
by the next screen, which displays the name of the user with a welcoming gesture and contains a total of
four sections. The three main sections are dedicated to the most frequently-used video calling
applications: Google Meet, Zoom, and Skype. Finally, the fourth section contains a fun mini-educational
quiz in which the user can test their knowledge on what they have learned from each calling application.
For example, when the user clicks on Zoom, they will be directed to a screen that displays six categories:
“Login,” “Start Meeting,” “Share Meeting,” “Join Meeting,” “Invite People,” and “Camera, Mic, and
Chat.” Each subsection explicitly shows how the user can utilize the respective functions. Above all, our
team wanted to emphasize the clarity of our application and how it can essentially be used by anyone who
is interested.
[Image of the various categories of my calling application, Help Grandpa. Photo courtesy of Sean Kang]
We ran into several challenges while programming our application: the hardest part was organizing the 28
different screens. More specifically, it was difficult to manage all of the button IDs (variables that indicate
what the user clicks on) and the screen IDs (variables that indicate which part of the application they are
on) so that they follow a consistent pattern and are distinct from one another.
After countless hours of coding and collaboration, our team created a demo video that gave a basic
overview of the application and explained its functions. We submitted it after two days of hard work, and
it was just a matter of time until we would be able to see our results. At 6 pm, the results were announced,
and our team was chosen as the Best Overall Application. We were all extremely surprised, because we
thought that our application was not as technologically advanced as some of the others we’d seen.
Overall, I have gained many essential lessons from the competition. One of the most crucial things I
learned was that coding is actually not that hard to learn, as it is just a matter of accumulating experience
and time to get used to the act of coding. Second, I realized that coding is just another outlet for
expressing creativity. Finally, I learned (the hard way!) that thinking outside the box is very important for
one to be successful or stand out in whatever they are doing. The “thinking outside of the box” mentality
not only helped us win first place, but also fully express my creativity through coding for the first time.
